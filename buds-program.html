BUD/S Preparation Program — 47 Weeks

How to use this

  1. Charts tab: find the current week, do what the row says.
  2. Sections tab: every workout name is explained there. Tap a section to open it. § numbers in the charts point to those sections.
  3. After every test, enter the scores in the Calibrate tab — it updates your paces, treadmill speeds, swim targets, and remaining gates automatically.
  4. When something goes wrong — missed days, sickness, pain, a failed target — the Adjust tab applies the plan’s rules to your situation and outputs the decision. No outside input needed.
  5. Any word you do not recognize — an exercise, a term, an abbreviation — is defined in the Glossary tab, which has a search box.
  6. The Science tab explains how to run each session type correctly (intensity zones, the 4×4 interval method, swim mechanics, hypertrophy dose). Read it once before Week 1 — it changes how the sessions are executed, not just why.

A heart-rate monitor (chest strap) is required equipment for this version. Intensity zones are set by heart rate; running easy days too hard and hard days too easy is the most common way endurance plans fail (Science tab, A).

The two phases, explained

Phase A — Weeks 1–21 (Jul 6 – Nov 29). Build the base while gaining weight.
Lifting stays the main focus (3 gym days a week) and you keep eating extra to grow toward ~175–180 lb. Underneath the bulk, running and swimming start very small and grow slowly, so your bones, tendons, and swim stroke are ready before the hard work begins. Phase A ends with Test 4 on Nov 28.

Phase B — Weeks 22–47 (Nov 30 – May 27). Slim down while getting fast.
Running and swimming become the main job. Lifting drops to one short full-body day a week (to keep the muscle you built). You eat a little less each day to come down toward 158–165 lb. From Week 26, every weekday morning starts with the push-up + pull-up routine. Phase B ends with the final test on May 22 and the fin-swim test on May 25.

Inside each phase, weeks are grouped in blocks of about 4. The last week of most blocks is a deload — a planned easy week. Deloads are part of the program, not a break from it.

Equipment

  • Garmin watch: wrist heart rate is accurate enough for easy runs at steady pace — cap easy days by HR. During intervals, wrist HR lags and misreads; run intervals by Garmin pace + the talk test instead. A chest strap that pairs to the watch fixes this; useful, not mandatory.
  • Intervals without a track: flat empty street. Measure a 400m stretch once with the watch, or run repeats by time from the pace table. The 4x4 sessions are time-based anyway.
  • Treadmill (gym): usable for interval sessions and bad-weather easy runs, with the rules in the Sections tab (2): 1% incline always, at most half of weekly running indoors, all tests and benchmarks outdoors.
  • Rigid fins (rocket/jet type): buy by Week 16. Fin work starts Week 17.
  • Already covered: pool, boots. Add: two pairs of running shoes (rotate), a short rope for knots (Phase B), a pull-up bar.

Words to know

  • Easy run — slow enough to speak in full sentences the entire time. If not, slow down. Most weekly running is at this speed. This is intentional.
  • Bursts — after an easy run: 4–6 runs of ~20 seconds, fast but controlled, full walk-back rest between.
  • Speed play — inside a run: 1 minute faster, 1 minute slow jog, repeated. "8×1" = 8 rounds.
  • Track 6×400 — interval repeats. No track needed: use the Garmin on a flat, empty street. Either measure a 400m stretch once and reuse it, or run repeats by time (the pace table gives the exact time per repeat, e.g. 1:43 per 400m at an 11:00 baseline). 60–90 seconds slow jog between. Every repeat the same speed.
  • Hard run — a VO2max interval session, run as the 4×4 protocol (four 4-minute efforts at 90–95% max heart rate, 3 min easy jog between). This is the highest-value endurance session in the plan. Full method on the Science tab (B).
  • Long run — longest easy run of the week.
  • Boots run — easy run in military-style boots on grass or sand. Never fast.
  • Deload week — a planned easy week. Fitness is built during recovery. Do only what is written.
  • Test — full practice of the Navy Physical Screening Test. Exact procedure in §15.
  • Goal pace — the speed that produces your target time. Example: an 8:20 swim = 50 yards every 50 seconds. A 9:00 run = 90 seconds per 400m lap.
  • Maintenance calories — the amount of food that keeps weight the same. Phase A = eat 300–500 above it. Phase B = eat 300–400 below it.

Treadmill rules. The treadmill is a precision tool for intervals and a backup for weather — not a replacement for ground running.

  • Always set 1% incline. At interval speeds, 1% grade reproduces the energy cost of outdoor running (no air resistance on a belt). Flat treadmill running is measurably easier than the road.
  • Speed settings: the Calibrate tab computes your current easy-run and interval mph from your latest test — use those numbers. Trust heart rate and the talk test over the display; belts are often miscalibrated.
  • 4×4 on incline: during high-volume or deficit weeks, run the 4×4 at 3–5% incline and a slower speed — heart rate still reaches 90–95% with far less impact on the legs.
  • Limits: no more than ~half of weekly running indoors. All tests, benchmarks, and boots runs happen outdoors. From Week 34 on, move interval days outdoors too — the belt holds pace for you, and the skill being trained is holding it yourself. The test is run on ground.

Intensity is set by heart rate, not feel. Easy runs are Zone 1 (<75% HRmax) — hold it even if it feels too slow. "Hard run" days are VO2max intervals run as the 4×4 protocol in Zone 3 (90–95% HRmax). Track repeats are Zone 3. There is almost no Zone 2 "moderate" running in this plan by design. Full zone table and reasoning: Science tab (A) and (B).

  • "Hard run" in the charts = the 4×4 session (Science tab B): four 4-min efforts at 90–95% HRmax, 3 min easy jog between, after a 10-min warm-up. Twice a week maximum. On beaten-up legs, substitute short intervals (15/15 or 30/30) or run the 4×4 on an incline.
  • Track laps are run at goal pace exactly — not faster, not slower. The purpose is to memorize how goal pace feels (breathing, stride, arm swing) so it can be reproduced on test day within the first 100m.
  • Cadence: ~170–180 short, quiet steps per minute on all runs (count steps for 30 seconds occasionally). Quicker, lighter steps reduce per-step bone and joint load — relevant to the stress-fracture profile in Section 12.
  • Weekly mileage increases no more than ~10% at a time. In 874 GPS-tracked novice runners, jumps above 30% raised injury risk versus staying under 10%; and simply halving beginners’ starting distance cut running injuries by roughly a third in a randomized trial. Small early weeks are the protective dose (Science tab, G).
  • Two pairs of running shoes, rotated. Replace a pair after 300–500 miles. Track shoe mileage in the logbook.
  • Soft surfaces (grass, dirt, track) when available.
  • Boots: buy military-style boots, wet them, and walk in them for a week before the first boots run. Boots runs stay easy and stay on grass or sand.
  • Heat (July–September): run early morning. Expect heart rate 5–10 bpm higher at the same pace in heat — trust the talk test over the zone numbers on hot days, and add salt and water around sessions. Heat-adapted training raises blood plasma volume; the fitness carries into fall. Move a session to the treadmill only when conditions are genuinely unsafe, not merely uncomfortable.
  • Sharp pain in a bone (shin, top of foot, hip): stop that run. See §12.
§3 Swimming +

Standards to reach:

  1. Cross a 25-yd pool in 5–6 strokes (counting the push-off glide). Count strokes every session.
  2. Hold 50 yards in 50 seconds. Held for 500 yards, that is 8:20. Pace checkpoints: 100 yd = 1:40, 200 = 3:20, 300 = 5:00.

Volume: getting the 500 into the low-8 range requires swimming most days. From Week 26, every session is at least 1,000 yards; documented programs that produced 10:00→8:00 improvements used 1,000–1,500 yards per session, 4–6 days per week. The optional 5th swim exists for this reason. More swim volume is the one place where adding is allowed, if sleep and morning heart rate stay normal.

The stroke (Combat Sidestroke). One cycle = pull → breathe → kick → glide:

  1. Lie on your side, bottom arm extended forward, ear on that shoulder, top arm on the hip. Body long and flat.
  2. Pull: the top arm sweeps down to the hip; turn the mouth up and breathe during the pull.
  3. Kick: scissor — top leg forward, bottom leg back — snap together. Small, no splash.
  4. Glide 2–3 seconds. Head stays low.

Off every wall: underwater push-off in a stretched position, one double-arm pull, then surface.

Reference: the official Combat Sidestroke guide and videos at sealswcc.com. Four to six sessions with any qualified swim coach will fix faults faster than self-correction.

Monthly video check (first Friday of each month): film the stroke side-on from deck level, one length. Compare against the official CSS video. Identify the single largest fault — usually head position, a rushed glide, or a wide kick — and make it the swim-skills focus for that month. An 11:00 500 at your size is a technique number, not a fitness number; this check is how the technique debt gets found and paid.

Workouts named in the charts:

  • Swim skills: lengths with one focus at a time (glide only, kick only). Track stroke count.
  • 50-50 ×10: 50 yd freestyle fast, then 50 yd sidestroke at goal pace with no rest between them; rest 3–4 breaths (~10 sec); repeat 10 times.
  • Goal-pace sets (5×100, 3×200): at 50-sec-per-50 pace, 20–30 seconds rest.
  • Nonstop swims: continuous. Early weeks may alternate sidestroke and freestyle by 100s; from Week 30, mostly sidestroke.
  • Swim-PT ×10: 100 yd swim, exit, 15–20 push-ups + 25 sit-ups, re-enter. Ten rounds.
  • Fins 75/25: stiff scuba-style fins (rocket/jet type). 75 yd sidestroke + 25 yd on the back, easy kick, no stopping. Kick from the hips, knees nearly straight. Daily ankle stretching once fin work starts.
  • Tread: vertical, light kick. Build to 5 minutes with hands out of the water.

Diagnostic rule: when stroke count on a length rises more than 2 above normal, that distance is the current failure point. Give that distance 5–6 repeats in the next block of sessions. During tests, have someone record the time for every 50 yards; the lap where pace drops shows where conditioning ends.

From Week 34: hard swim days open with a nonstop 500-yd warm-up at easy pace. Purpose: the test distance becomes routine.

Swim stalled? The Adjust tab has a fault finder that maps symptoms to the exact fix drill.

Safety rule, no exceptions: breath-holding and underwater work only with a coach or lifeguard actively watching. Never alone. Never after rapid deep breathing. Open water always with a partner. Shallow-water blackout occurs without warning.
§4 Test execution strategy +

The most common cause of underperformance on the PST is pacing error, not fitness.

  • Swim: even 50-yd splits at goal pace from the first lap. Do not open faster than goal pace. A fast first 100 costs more time at the end than it gains at the start, and drains the other four events.
  • Push-ups: hands placed level with the chest, just outside shoulder width. Hands too high or too low reduces reps.
  • Sit-ups: treat 2 minutes as four 30-second blocks. For an 80–100 target: 20–25 reps per block. Do not open with 30–35 in the first block. If failing near the end, slide the hips back 2–3 inches; the angle change typically allows 5–10 more reps.
  • Pull-ups: steady rhythm; no time limit, so short pauses at the top of the hang are allowed. Do not rush.
  • Rep style on test day: let gravity lower the body on push-ups, sit-ups, and pull-ups — exert only on the way up. (Training is the opposite: controlled lowering builds strength. Test day is for saving energy.)
  • The 10-minute break before the run: 1) stretch arms, chest, shoulders, stomach; 2) jog easy 2–3 minutes to move blood to the legs; 3) stretch legs 3–5 minutes; keep shaking the arms out. Blood pooled in the upper body after PT is why the first half-mile feels the worst; this procedure reduces it.
  • Run: settle onto goal pace within the first 100m. Even splits. Do not open the first lap fast.
  • Fuel: hydrate normally beforehand; sip water with electrolytes and some carbohydrate (diluted sports drink) during the breaks, especially before the run.
  • From Test 5 on, wear the same clothes and follow the same meal and warm-up every test, so the final one contains nothing new.
§5 Pull-ups +

Until 8 strict reps (Phase A): Mon/Wed/Fri, 5–8 small sets of about half of max, spread across the day, never to failure. Add slow lower-downs: jump to the top, lower for 5 seconds.

From Week 26, with 8+ strict reps — Armstrong routine, every weekday morning after the push-up sets:

  • Mon: 5 max-effort sets, 90 sec rest.
  • Tue: pyramid — 1 rep, rest 10 sec; 2 reps, rest 20 sec; 3 reps, rest 30 sec; continue until a number is missed; rest; one final max set.
  • Wed: 9 sets of (max ÷ 3, rounded up), 60 sec rest: 3 sets normal grip, 3 sets close underhand grip, 3 sets wide grip.
  • Thu: same set size as Wed, as many sets as possible, 60 sec rest. The week this exceeds 9 sets, add 1 rep to the set size.
  • Fri: repeat the hardest day of the week.

Weekends off the bar. Deload weeks: 2 light days. If elbows or shoulders are sore two sessions in a row, drop one day and reduce set sizes for that week.

Form: full hang at the bottom, chin over the bar, shoulders pulled down before the arms bend, no swinging.

§6 Push-ups +

Morning routine (Phase B weekdays; Phase A Mon/Wed/Fri from Week 13): 3 max-effort sets spread over ~10 minutes.

Training form: body straight, chest to fist height, full lockout, controlled lowering. Reps that would not count at an official test are not counted in the log.

If shoulders ache: hands directly under shoulders, elbows closer to the body, and skip one morning.

§7 Sit-ups, Pyramid, Grinder +

Sit-ups: feet anchored, arms crossed on chest, up until elbows touch thighs, down until shoulder blades touch. Two 2-minute rounds at steady rhythm, using the 30-second block pacing from §4. Afterward: plank 3×45 sec, flutter kicks 3×20.

Pyramid: count 1-2-3-4-5-4-3-2-1. Each number = that many pull-ups, ×2 push-ups, ×3 sit-ups. Progress toward a peak of 10 by Weeks 39–43. Minimal rest.

Grinder: 100 pull-ups, then 200 push-ups, then 300 sit-ups, each in as few sets as possible, with a 400m run (~90–100 sec) between every set. Week 37 is half size; Week 41 is full size. Eat and sleep normally the day before; treat it as a dress rehearsal for BUD/S-style volume.

§8 Grip, obstacle prep, knots +

Twice a week, ~5 minutes after lifting:

  • Bar hang: 2 rounds, maximum time; build toward 60–90 seconds
  • Towel over the bar, hang or rows: 2 rounds
  • Heavy dumbbell carries: 3 walks of ~40 steps
  • Once a week if available: monkey bars (2–3 passes) or a climbing rope (legs do the work: trap the rope between the feet, stand up, re-grip)

Knots: First Phase at BUD/S includes an underwater knot-tying test on five knots — bowline, square knot, becket's bend, clove hitch, and right angle. Learn all five on dry land now: two 5-minute practice blocks per week with a short piece of rope, starting Phase B. Goal by May: tie each one in under 30 seconds without looking. Free instructions and videos: search "BUD/S five knots" (Art of Manliness and ITS Tactical both have step-by-step guides).

The underwater version is trained only at BUD/S under instructors. Do not self-practice the underwater part.
§9 Lifting +

Phase A — Lift A / B / C, full-body days (Mon / Wed / Fri). Not push-pull-legs: each day trains the whole body with a different emphasis, so every muscle is hit 2–3 times per week — the frequency the hypertrophy research supports (Science tab, D) — and weekly volume lands at roughly 9–14 hard sets per muscle. Stop compound lifts 1–3 reps before failure; take isolation work closer to failure. Progression: when the top of a rep range is reached with good form, add weight.

  • Lift A (Mon) — squat emphasis: squat 4×5–6 · bench press 4×6–8 · one-arm dumbbell row 3×8–10 · Romanian deadlift 3×8 · lateral raises 3×12–15 · curls 2×10–12 · calf raises 3×12
  • Lift B (Wed) — press emphasis: overhead press 4×6–8 · weighted pull-ups 4×5–8 · walking lunges 3×10 per leg · incline dumbbell press 3×8–10 · barbell row 3×8 · triceps 2×10–12 · toe raises 3×15 · light neck work
  • Lift C (Fri) — hinge/pull emphasis: trap-bar or conventional deadlift 3×5 · dips 3×8–12 · chin-ups 3×8–10 · Bulgarian split squats 2×10 per leg · dumbbell bench 3×10 · face pulls 3×15 · curls 2×12 · calf raises 3×12

Ordering rule: the deadlift and squat stay at moderate effort (never grinding reps) because runs follow the next morning. If Thursday speed play feels dead-legged two weeks in a row, lighten Wednesday lunges.

An existing lifting program may be substituted, provided calf raises, toe raises, neck work, and carries stay in.

Phase B, one 40-minute full-body day: squats 3×5 · shoulder press 3×6–8 · rows 3×8 · lunges 2×10 per leg · heavy carries 3×40 steps · calves, toes, neck. Weight stays heavy, total sets stay low. Purpose is keeping muscle during the cut, not building.

These lifting days are also the injury-prevention program: in meta-analysis of randomized trials, strength training cut sports injuries to less than a third and nearly halved overuse injuries — protection Phase B’s running load will spend. Stretching remains in the plan for mobility and fin flexibility, not injury statistics (Science tab, G).

Strength benchmarks by May (durability reference, not a priority in the final 6 weeks): deadlift ~1.5× bodyweight for 3 reps; squat ~1.25× bodyweight for 5; carry half of bodyweight per hand for 40 steps.

§10 Food +

Phase A: 300–500 calories above maintenance. Protein 150–170 g/day. Weigh 3 mornings a week; use the weekly average; gain no more than ~1 lb/week. In the randomized athlete weight-gain trial, a guided ~500 kcal surplus added weight faster than eating freely — but the difference was fat, not muscle. The +300–500 cap is where the evidence says useful stops (Science tab, G). Note: 153→180 by Week 21 requires ~1.3 lb/week, which adds meaningful fat. Arriving at Week 22 at 172–176 with less fat is an equal or better outcome.

Phase B: Weeks 22–23 at maintenance — two purposes: the body stabilizes after five months of surplus, and logged intake at stable weight pins the true maintenance number the deficit is measured from. From Week 24: 300–400 below that number — the slow-loss protocol shown to preserve muscle and strength in athletes. Hard cap: never lose faster than 0.7% of bodyweight per week (~1.2 lb/week at 175 lb). Approximate intake 2,900–3,200; not below ~2,800 during Weeks 34–43. Protein RISES in a deficit: 2.2–2.4 g per kg — practically 175–190 g/day (higher protein during energy restriction demonstrably protects muscle; above ~2.4 g/kg adds nothing). Carbohydrates placed around the biggest sessions. Deload weeks, Week 38, and Weeks 45–47: maintenance.

Bone support: in a randomized trial of 5,201 Navy recruits, 2,000 mg calcium + 800 IU vitamin D daily reduced stress fractures by 20% in 8 weeks of basic training. Floor: 1,300 mg calcium/day from food (3–4 dairy or fortified servings). Through Phase B — deficit plus peak mileage plus winter — a daily calcium + vitamin D supplement is directly evidence-supported; confirm dose at the sports physical.

Example Phase B day (~3,000 cal, ~170 g protein): breakfast — 4 eggs, 1 cup oats with milk and a banana. Lunch — 8 oz chicken, 1.5 cups rice, vegetables, olive oil. Pre-workout — Greek yogurt and fruit. Dinner — 8 oz ground beef or fish, potatoes, vegetables. Evening — cottage cheese or a peanut-butter sandwich and milk. Adjust portions to hit the weekly weight trend.

Stop-the-cut signals — if two appear, eat at maintenance for 1–2 weeks: times regress for 2+ weeks · morning heart rate up 5–8 beats for 3+ days · feeling cold constantly · broken sleep · accumulating small injuries · aversion to every session.

Supplements: none required. Creatine 3–5 g/day is legal and supported by evidence, optional. Nothing else. NSW drug-tests; a positive test ends the process.

Hydration: water through the day; add electrolytes around long or hot sessions.

§11 Breathing +
  • Easy runs: in 3 steps, out 3 steps. Hard runs and track: in 2, out 2.
  • Swimming: exhale continuously whenever the face is in the water; the surface moment is inhale only.
  • Box breathing, nightly 5 minutes and between test events: in 4 seconds, hold 4, out 4, hold 4.
  • Fast reset between hard reps: two quick nasal inhales, one long exhale.
  • After hard efforts: hands on head, nasal inhale, long exhale.

§12 Injury prevention and monitoring +

Roughly 1 in 5 BUD/S students develops a stress fracture; the main drivers reported by NSW medical staff are ramping too fast, poor recovery, and under-eating. This program controls all three; the items below are the remaining margin.

Circuit, twice a week after lifting (~10 min): slow-lowering calf raises 3×12 (1 sec up, 5 sec down) · bent-knee calf raises 2×15 · toe raises 3×15 · single-leg balance, eyes closed, 2×30 sec per leg · side-lying leg lifts 2×15 per side.

Bone loading (from Week 9): before two runs per week, 2×10 pogo hops on grass — stiff ankles, quiet landings, minimal knee bend. Brief jumping is one of the few proven bone-strengthening stimuli; directly relevant to a frame that gained 40+ lb quickly and is adding running load on top. Takes 90 seconds. Skip on deload weeks.

Daily 10-minute stretch: calves and ankles first (running and fins depend on them), hip flexors, hamstrings, chest doorway stretch, shoulder circles with a band. The free NSW Injury Prevention Guide (sealswcc.com) has a full illustrated series.

Morning heart rate: 3× per week, 60-second count before getting out of bed, logged. A rise of 5–8 beats for 3+ days = take an easy day and check sleep and food.

Stop rules: sharp bone pain while running — stop that run; swim/bike only until pain-free for 7 days; still present at 10 days — imaging. Limping, swelling, or altered stride — same. Elbow or shoulder pain from bar work two sessions in a row — remove a day.

Sports physical before December. Cold exposure: end showers with 1–3 minutes cold from Phase B onward. Cold open water only briefly and never alone.

§13 Sick days, missed days, substitutions +

Sick: symptoms above the neck only (runny nose, mild sore throat) — do the easy version or half the session. Fever, chest congestion, or body aches — full rest until 24 hours fever-free, then two easy days before resuming the chart.

Missed day: it is skipped, not made up. Never stack two hard run days back-to-back to compensate.

When time is short, priority order: 1) test days, 2) track and hard runs, 3) swims (minimum 3 per week, 1,000+ yd each), 4) lifting, 5) everything else. Sessions may move within the week if the hard-easy pattern is kept.

Missed target at a test: repeat the previous block's Saturday sessions for two weeks, retest. Injury during prep: physician, keep training whatever is pain-free, rebuild the run ladder from two weeks below the stopping point.

Why the plan is built this way

Every rule below traces to peer-reviewed research or official NSW material. This page is the engine under the charts. Nothing here is optional reading; it defines how to run the sessions correctly. Sources are numbered and listed at the bottom.

A Intensity zones — the core of everything +

Endurance training works only if intensity is controlled. The research-backed model is polarized (roughly 80/20): about 80% of weekly endurance time at genuinely easy intensity, about 20% hard, and almost nothing in the moderate middle. In controlled trials this outperformed threshold-focused and moderate-continuous training for VO2max and time-trial performance [1][2][3]. Most people train their easy days too hard and their hard days too easy, landing in an unproductive middle. Do not.

Three zones, set by heart rate. First estimate max heart rate (HRmax). Best: do a hard 4-minute effort test and note the highest HR seen. Rough fallback: 208 − (0.7 × age) = ~196 at age 18. Use a chest-strap monitor; wrist optical is inaccurate at high intensity.

Zone% HRmaxFeel / talk testUsed for
Z1 — Easy<75% (~145 bpm)Full conversation, nose-breathing possibleAll easy runs, long runs, recovery swims, warm-ups/cool-downs. ~80% of volume.
Z2 — Threshold82–88% (~160–172)3–4 words onlyUsed sparingly. Tempo/"hard run" pieces and goal-race-pace work. ~5%.
Z3 — VO2max90–95% (~176–186)Cannot speak4×4 intervals, track repeats. ~15%.

The rule this creates: on easy days, hold Z1 even if it feels absurdly slow — this is where the aerobic base, mitochondria, and capillaries are built, and where injury risk is lowest [1][3]. On hard days, reach the true top zone. The charts already schedule ~4 easy sessions per hard session; the heart-rate monitor is what keeps you honest inside each one.

B The 4×4 protocol — how "hard run" days are now run +

The most-validated single session for raising VO2max — the ceiling on both run speed and fin-swim endurance — is the Norwegian 4×4 (Helgerud et al. 2007): four 4-minute efforts at 90–95% HRmax, each followed by 3 minutes of easy jogging. It raised VO2max ~7–10% in 8 weeks and beat both moderate-continuous and lactate-threshold training, driven by a larger increase in the heart's stroke volume [4][5][6].

Execution, whenever the charts say "Hard run":

  1. Warm up 10 minutes easy (§1).
  2. 4 minutes at 90–95% HRmax. First interval may start slightly lower (~88%) since HR lags; by minute 2 you should be at target and unable to talk.
  3. 3 minutes easy jog (do not stop moving).
  4. Repeat for 4 total work intervals. Cool down 5–10 minutes.
  5. Pace target: the last interval covers the same distance as the first. If distance drops off, the early ones were too fast.

Frequency: 2 quality sessions per week is the validated dose; a third adds little and raises injury risk in a calorie deficit [7]. This is why the charts pair one 4×4 / track day with one shorter, sharper session, not three hard days.

Uphill option: running the 4×4 on a hill or treadmill incline lets the heart, not the legs, be the limiter, and lowers impact — useful during high-volume or deficit weeks [4].

Short-interval alternative (the track 15/15 or 30/30): 15–30 sec at ~95% HRmax, equal easy jog, repeated. Produces similar time-at-VO2max with less muscular strain [4][8]. Use it in place of a 4×4 when legs are beaten up but you still want quality.

C Swim speed — mechanics before conditioning +

Water resistance rises with roughly the square of speed, so in swimming, reducing drag returns more than adding power. Priorities, in order:

  1. Body line and drag: stay horizontal and long, head neutral (looking down/forward, not up), hips high. A dropped head sinks the legs and multiplies drag. This is the largest, cheapest time saving.
  2. Distance per stroke: the stroke-count metric (5–6 per 25 yd) is a direct drag-and-efficiency measure. Lengthen the glide; add strokes only when speed genuinely requires it.
  3. Streamline off every wall: a tight underwater push-off is faster than any surface swimming; in a 25-yd pool this is ~20 free accelerations per 500.
  4. Then conditioning: only once the stroke is efficient does raising aerobic and threshold swim fitness (the 50-50 and goal-pace sets) convert directly to a faster 500.

Combat Sidestroke specifics: it is an underwater-recovery stroke — arms recover below the surface, keeping a low profile and pairing efficiently with fins later. The scissor kick's power comes from a full leg spread then a hard snap-together, followed by a glide. Breathing is timed to the top-arm pull with continuous exhalation underwater; breath-holding creates tension and buoyancy loss. Fixing faults by video or coach is faster than self-correction [official CSS guide, 9].

Volume basis: documented 500-time drops from ~10:00 to ~8:00 used 1,000–1,500 yd per session, 4–6 days/week [9]. This is why swim frequency and the optional 5th swim exist; swim technique also decays fastest without frequent practice, so short frequent beats long infrequent.

D Muscle growth (Phase A) — evidence-based dose +

Given the goal of gaining from ~153 to ~180 while starting lean, hypertrophy is a Phase-A priority. The controlling variables from meta-analysis:

  • Weekly volume: ~10–20 hard sets per muscle group per week is the responsive range; growth rises ~0.2–0.4% per additional weekly set, plateauing and eventually reversing past ~20 as fatigue outpaces recovery [10][11][12]. Below ~5 sets is submaximal. The Phase-A lifting split is built to land each major muscle in this window.
  • Proximity to failure: sets should stop within ~0–4 reps of failure (rep ranges ~6–20 all work if effort is high) [11]. On big compound lifts, keep 1–3 in reserve to protect the running/swimming; on isolation work, push closer to failure.
  • Frequency: training each muscle ~2×/week matches or beats 1× at equal volume, mainly by splitting volume into more productive sessions [13].
  • Protein: ~1.6 g/kg/day maximizes the muscle-building response; more is not harmful but adds little. At ~70–82 kg that is ~115–170 g/day — the plan's 150–170 g covers it with margin [14].
  • Energy: a surplus of ~300–500 kcal/day maximizes muscle-to-fat gain ratio; larger surpluses add mostly fat you will later have to cut [general nutrition consensus].

Progression: add weight or reps when the top of a rep range is hit with good form (double progression). Log every lift; the load must trend up over Phase A or growth stalls.

E Concurrent training — protecting gains from interference +

Training endurance and strength together can blunt each (the "interference effect"), strongest when high-volume running and leg lifting collide. Research-supported ways to minimize it, all built into the charts:

  • Separate hard endurance and hard lifting by time. Ideally 6+ hours; at minimum, don't do a hard leg lift and a hard run in the same session. Where the charts stack AM/PM, the hard endurance piece and the lift are split across the day.
  • Run before you would ever lift on a shared day is avoided; when both fall together, lifting quality is protected because Phase B lifting is low-volume maintenance, not growth work.
  • Phase separation is the main lever: Phase A prioritizes hypertrophy with running kept low; Phase B flips to endurance with lifting cut to one maintenance session that preserves, not builds, muscle. This sequencing sidesteps most interference [general concurrent-training literature].
  • Cycling/incline options for the 4×4 reduce leg damage on weeks where running volume is already high, preserving lifting output.
F Periodization and recovery — why deloads and the taper exist +

Progressive overload with planned recovery: fitness is built when a training stress is followed by adequate recovery (supercompensation). Unbroken hard training without recovery produces stagnation or overtraining. The ~every-4th-week deload (volume cut ~40%, intensity kept) is the scheduled recovery that lets adaptation surface — a standard periodization tool, not lost time.

Taper: the meta-analytic optimum is a ~2-week taper cutting volume 41–60% while keeping intensity and session frequency unchanged, ideally after an overload block — worth roughly 3% on test day (details and sources in block H). Weeks 39–43 are the overload; Weeks 44–46 are the taper; May 22 is the payout.

Sleep is the largest recovery and adaptation variable available: it drives growth-hormone release, muscle repair, and motor-skill consolidation (the swim stroke and pacing "lock in" partly during sleep). 8–9 hours is a performance input, not downtime [general sleep-performance literature].

Monitoring: a resting heart rate elevated 5–8 bpm for several days, or times regressing for 2+ weeks, signals under-recovery — the trigger to add a maintenance-calorie/easy period. This is objective feedback, logged, not guesswork.

G Phase A: building the base without buying fat or injuries +

Surplus size — the weight-gain trial. In a randomized study of elite athletes gaining weight (guided ~500 kcal/day surplus + 4 lifting sessions/week vs. eating freely), the guided group gained bodyweight faster (+3.9% vs +1.5%) — but the difference was fat (fat mass +15% vs +3%); short-term LEAN gain was not different between groups [19]. The 12-month follow-up showed what wins: the group that held the modest structured surplus consistently ended +4.4% lean mass versus no change [19]. Coded into the plan: the surplus is capped at +300–500 because calories beyond that demonstrably buy fat, and the lean-mass race is won by holding a modest surplus for months, not by eating bigger.

The run ramp — novice data. In 874 GPS-tracked novice runners followed for a year, increasing weekly distance by more than 30% raised distance-injury risk versus staying under 10% [20]. A randomized trial that simply halved beginners' starting weekly distance cut running-related injuries by roughly a third and overuse symptoms by nearly half within 4 weeks [20]. This is why Weeks 1–4 start at distances that feel insultingly short: they are the protective dose, not lost time.

Sleep — an injury variable, measured. Tracking 112 adolescent athletes against 21 months of injury records: those sleeping under 8 hours a night carried 1.7× the injury rate, and sleeping 8+ hours was associated with 68% lower injury odds [21]. In your age group, the nightly 8–9 hours in Daily Habits is one of the largest single injury-risk levers that exists — which is why it is written as a rule.

Lifting is the armor, not just the engine. Meta-analysis of randomized trials (~26,600 participants): strength training cut sports injuries to less than one-third (RR 0.315) and nearly halved overuse injuries; the effect is dose-dependent. Stretching alone prevented nothing [22] — it stays in the plan for mobility and fin flexibility, not injury statistics. Phase A's three lifting days therefore serve double duty: they build the mass, and they are the documented injury-prevention program for the running load that Phase B will spend. Supervised barbell training at this age is consensus-supported as safe with proper technique.

H Phase B: cutting weight without losing performance +

Rate of loss (the controlling variable). In a randomized trial of elite athletes, a slow cut of 0.7% bodyweight per week (~470 kcal/day deficit) produced a 2.1% GAIN in lean mass and ~12% squat-strength improvement during the cut, while 1.4%/week (~845 kcal deficit) produced no lean gain and worse performance outcomes [15]. Rule: never exceed 0.7% of bodyweight per week — at ~175 lb that is ~1.2 lb/week maximum. The plan's −300 to −400 deficit sits inside the validated slow protocol.

Protein must rise in a deficit. Athletes cutting on 2.3 g/kg/day lost almost no lean mass (−0.3 kg) versus −1.6 kg on 1.0 g/kg with identical fat loss [16]. A 2.4 g/kg intake inside a large deficit preserved muscle AND removed more fat than 1.2 g/kg [16]. Systematic review places the deficit requirement at 1.8–2.7 g/kg, with no additional benefit above ~2.4 [16]. Phase B target: 2.2–2.4 g/kg — practically 175–190 g/day.

The taper, quantified. Meta-analysis of 27 studies: the optimal pre-test strategy is a ~2-week taper cutting volume 41–60% while leaving intensity and session frequency UNCHANGED [17]. A 2023 meta-analysis adds two Phase-B-relevant findings: a taper preceded by an overload block (exactly what Weeks 39–43 are) produces larger gains, and maintaining session frequency matters most in technique-dependent sports — i.e., swimming — which is why Weeks 44–46 keep short, frequent swims rather than dropping the pool [17].

Bone protection during the deficit. In a randomized, double-blind trial of 5,201 Navy recruits in basic training, 2,000 mg calcium + 800 IU vitamin D daily cut stress-fracture incidence by 20% in 8 weeks [18]. Phase B stacks the three fracture drivers (mileage peak, deficit, winter/low sun); dietary calcium at the 1,300 mg/day age-18 requirement is the floor, and a daily calcium + vitamin D supplement through Phase B is directly evidence-supported — confirm the dose at the sports physical.

I Expected adaptations under full execution +

Assumptions: every session executed as written, 8–9 h sleep, calories per plan, no skipped deloads, no injury or illness. Under those conditions, the documented response ranges are:

  • Aerobic capacity: the 4×4 protocol at 2 sessions/week produced ~7–10% VO2max gains in 8 weeks in controlled trials; untrained-to-novice runners respond at the high end. Phase B contains ~4 such cycles with deloads. The run gate path (11:00 → ≤8:50 over 46 weeks, ~3 sec/week average) sits well inside these documented adaptation rates.
  • 500-yd swim: the reference program (1,000–1,500 yd/session, 4–6 days/week, 50-50 method) documented ~10:00 → ~8:00 in roughly one month in prep candidates. This plan allots 45 weeks at that frequency for a smaller total drop (11:00 → 8:00–8:15). The swim gate is the most conservatively set target in the program because the event is technique-dominant and the volume dose matches the documented one several times over.
  • Pull-ups: the Armstrong program's documented outcome is 20+ strict reps typically within 6–8 weeks for trainees starting near 8–12. The plan allots 21 weeks of build-up plus 22 weeks of the program itself before the 20+ gate.
  • Push-ups / sit-ups: the gate path requires ~1.1 reps/week average. Daily submaximal-volume protocols in military-prep populations routinely produce 1–2 reps/week in the first months, slowing near the 90s — which is why the curve is front-loaded shallow and the final standard is 95–100, not higher.
  • Muscle mass (Phase A): at 10–20 hard sets/muscle/week, ~2× weekly frequency, ~1.6 g/kg protein, and a 300–500 kcal surplus, early-stage trainees add lean mass at roughly 0.5–1% of bodyweight per month; gain rates above ~1 lb/week shift the surplus toward fat. The 153 → mid-170s path over 21 weeks sits at the aggressive edge of the lean range — expected composition: majority lean at ≤1 lb/week, progressively fattier above it.
  • Strength: squat/deadlift progressing to the 1.25–1.5× bodyweight benchmarks is the standard first-year trajectory for a novice on 3×/week full-body progression.
  • Injury risk: the three drivers NSW medical staff cite for the ~1-in-5 BUD/S stress-fracture rate — erratic load ramps, poor recovery, under-fueling — are each controlled by a specific mechanism in this plan (10% rule + deloads; sleep/HR monitoring; calorie floors). Risk is managed, not zero; the bone-loading and Run-Armor work exist to widen that margin.

Biology returns ranges, not points, even at perfect adherence — that is a property of physiology, not of effort. The strongest factual statement available: every gate in this program is set inside, and mostly below, the adaptation rates documented in the cited research and prep-program data.

Glossary — every term, exercise, and method

Type any word from the plan to find it. Everything in the program is defined here in plain language.

Units and basics

mm:ss — minutes and seconds. 8:30 means 8 minutes 30 seconds.
yd / m — yards / meters. Most pools are 25 yards per length. 400m is about one lap of a track, or a quarter mile.
mph — miles per hour, the treadmill speed setting.
bpm — heart beats per minute.
Rep — one complete movement (one push-up, one squat).
Set — a group of reps done back-to-back before resting. "3×12" means 3 sets of 12 reps.
Max-effort set — as many clean reps as possible in one set.
1–3 reps before failure — stop the set while you could still do 1–3 more clean reps. You should never grind to a total stop on compound lifts in this plan.
Double progression — work within a rep range (say 6–8). When all sets hit 8 with good form, add a little weight and start again at 6.
Pace — time per mile (running) or per 50 yards (swimming). Lower = faster.
Splits — your time at each checkpoint. Swim splits = time every 50 yards; they show where you slow down.
Baseline — your first measured score. Everything is compared against it.
Benchmark — a first-time measurement of an extra event (4-mile run, fin swim, tread) so later targets have a real starting point.
Gate / target — the score you should reach by a given test date to stay on the final-standard path.
HRmax — your maximum heart rate. Estimate: 208 − (0.7 × age) ≈ 196 at age 18, or the highest number seen during an all-out 4-minute effort.
Zone 1 / 2 / 3 — easy (<75% HRmax, full sentences), threshold (82–88%, 3–4 words), and VO2max (90–95%, can't talk). The plan lives almost entirely in Zones 1 and 3.
VO2max — the maximum rate your body can use oxygen. The ceiling on endurance performance; the 4×4 sessions exist to raise it.
Talk test — checking intensity by speech: full sentences = easy; 3–4 words = hard; can't speak = maximal.
Deload — a planned easy week (about 60% less work). Fitness is built during recovery; deloads are where gains lock in. Do only what's written.
Taper — the final weeks before the last test where volume drops 30–50% so you test fresh. Worth several percent on every event.
Maintenance calories — the daily food amount that keeps your weight the same.
Surplus / deficit — eating above maintenance (gain weight) / below it (lose weight). Phase A: +300–500. Phase B from Week 24: −300–400.
Protein grams — daily protein target (150–180 g). Reference: a chicken breast ≈ 40 g, 4 eggs ≈ 24 g, a cup of Greek yogurt ≈ 20 g.
Creatine — a legal, well-studied supplement (3–5 g daily) that slightly improves strength and muscle gain. Optional.
Electrolytes — salts (mainly sodium) lost in sweat. Replace with sports drink or salted food around long or hot sessions.
Morning heart rate — pulse counted for 60 seconds before getting out of bed. A rise of 5–8 bpm for 3+ days = under-recovered; take an easy day.

Running

Easy run — jogging slow enough to speak full sentences the whole time (Zone 1). If in doubt, slower. This builds the engine and the bone; it is most of your running on purpose.
Bursts (strides) — after an easy run: 4–6 relaxed sprints of ~20 seconds at fast-but-smooth speed, walking back fully between each. Trains speed and form cheaply.
Speed play (fartlek) — during a run: 1 minute faster, 1 minute slow jog, repeated. "8×1" = 8 rounds. A gentle introduction to speed.
4×4 — the main hard session: 4 minutes at 90–95% HRmax, 3 minutes easy jog, repeated 4 times, after a 10-minute warm-up. Pace the first interval so the last one covers the same distance.
Repeats (6×400, 5×800) — run a set distance at goal pace, jog 60–90 sec (400s) or 2:30–3:00 (800s), repeat. Every repeat the same speed — the point is memorizing goal pace.
Hill sprints — 45-second hard efforts up a hill, walk down to recover. Strength and speed with less impact.
Long run — the longest easy run of the week. Zone 1 the whole way.
Boots run — an easy run in military-style boots on grass or sand only, never fast. Conditions feet and ankles for BUD/S. Soak and walk in new boots for a week first.
Cadence — steps per minute. Aim ~170–180 short, quiet steps; count for 30 seconds occasionally. Lighter, quicker steps lower bone stress.
10% rule — never increase weekly running distance by more than about 10% at once. The main defense against stress fractures.
Pogo hops — small, quick, straight-leg bounces in place on grass, landing quietly on the balls of the feet. 2×10 before two runs a week from Week 9; strengthens bone and tendons.
1% incline — the treadmill setting that matches outdoor effort (a belt has no air resistance). Always on.

Swimming

Combat Sidestroke (CSS) — the Navy's test stroke, swum on your side: pull → breathe → kick → glide. Full teaching steps in Sections tab, 3, plus official videos at sealswcc.com.
Freestyle — regular front crawl: face down, arms alternating over the water, flutter kick. Used for the fast half of 50-50s.
Breaststroke — frog-style stroke; the only legal test alternative to sidestroke.
Glide — the 2–3 second slide after each kick with the body long and still. Free distance; rushing it is the #1 fault.
Stroke count — how many strokes one pool length takes. Target: 5–6 per 25 yd including push-off. Count every session; fewer = more efficient.
Streamline push-off — off every wall: push off underwater, arms squeezed straight overhead, body arrow-thin, one big double-arm pull, then surface.
Scissor kick — the sidestroke kick: top leg forward, bottom leg back, snap together, glide. Narrow, no splash.
Turtleback — swimming on your back with an easy kick to catch your breath. The recovery part of fin 75/25s.
Tread — staying vertical in deep water with a gentle kick. "Eggbeater" = legs circling alternately, the most efficient tread. Build to 5 minutes with hands out of the water.
50-50 — 50 yd freestyle fast, immediately 50 yd sidestroke at goal pace, rest 3–4 breaths, repeat. Teaches the stroke to survive being out of breath — the documented 500-time dropper.
Goal-pace sets — 100s or 200s swum at exactly the pace your next target time requires (the Calibrate tab computes it), 20–30 sec rest.
Nonstop swim — one continuous swim of the listed yards. No wall rests.
Swim-PT — 100 yd swim, climb out, 15–20 push-ups + 25 sit-ups on deck, back in. Ten rounds.
Fins 75/25 — with stiff scuba fins: 75 yd sidestroke + 25 yd turtleback, no stopping, repeated. Kick from the hips, knees nearly straight. Stretch ankles daily once fins start.
Rigid fins (rocket/jet type) — stiff rubber scuba fins, the kind BUD/S uses. Not flexible snorkeling flippers.
Drag — water resistance. It grows with the square of speed, which is why body position beats muscle in swimming.

Gym exercises — how to do each one

Squat — bar on upper back, feet shoulder-width. Sit down between your heels until thighs reach parallel, chest up, then stand. Knees track over toes.
Bench press — lying on a bench, lower the bar to mid-chest under control, press to straight arms. Feet planted, shoulder blades pinched back.
Incline dumbbell press — bench set to ~30–45°. Press dumbbells from shoulder level to straight arms. Hits upper chest.
Dumbbell bench press — flat-bench press with dumbbells instead of a bar; deeper stretch, each side works alone.
Overhead press — standing, bar at shoulders, press straight overhead to locked arms without leaning back. Squeeze glutes for a stable base.
Dips — on parallel bars, lower until upper arms reach parallel, press back up. Slight forward lean. Add weight only when 12 clean reps are easy.
Barbell row — hinge forward ~45° with flat back, pull the bar to your lower ribs, lower under control. No jerking.
One-arm dumbbell row — one hand and knee on a bench, back flat, pull the dumbbell to your hip, elbow along the ribs.
Pull-up grips — normal: palms away, shoulder-width. Close underhand (chin-up): palms toward you, hands close — more biceps. Wide: palms away, hands wide — more upper back.
Weighted pull-up — pull-up with a plate on a dip belt or a dumbbell between the feet. Same strict form: dead hang to chin over.
Face pull — cable or band at face height; pull toward your face with elbows high, squeezing shoulder blades. Keeps shoulders healthy under all this pressing.
Curls — dumbbells or bar, elbows pinned at your sides, curl to shoulders, lower slowly. No swinging.
Triceps work — cable pushdowns (press the bar down until arms straight, elbows pinned) or overhead dumbbell extensions.
Lateral raise — dumbbells at your sides, raise to shoulder height with slightly bent elbows, lower slowly. Light weight, strict.
Romanian deadlift (RDL) — holding the bar, push hips back with knees only slightly bent, lower the bar along the thighs to mid-shin with a flat back, drive hips forward to stand. Hamstrings and glutes.
Deadlift (conventional) — bar on the floor over mid-foot. Flat back, brace, stand up by driving the floor away. Lower under control. Never grind reps in this plan.
Trap-bar deadlift — same lift inside a hexagon bar with neutral handles; easier on the back, fine substitute.
Walking lunge — step forward, lower until the back knee nearly touches the floor, drive up into the next step. Torso tall.
Bulgarian split squat — rear foot elevated on a bench behind you; lower straight down on the front leg, drive up. Brutal and effective on one leg at a time.
Calf raise — on a step, rise onto the balls of the feet, pause, lower until heels dip below the step.
Slow-lowering calf raise — same, but 1 second up and a strict 5 seconds down. The slow lowering is what builds tendon strength.
Bent-knee calf raise — calf raise with knees bent ~30° (seated or leaning). Targets the soleus, the deep calf muscle running lives on.
Toe raise (tibialis) — heels on the ground, lift the toes toward the shins, lower slowly. Protects the shins from running load.
Neck work — lying down, lift the head against light hand pressure: front, back, each side, ~2×15 gently. At BUD/S, boats are carried on heads.
Farmer carry — pick up the heaviest dumbbells you can hold, stand tall, walk ~40 steps. Grip, core, and posture in one move.
Dead hang / towel hang — hang from the bar (or from a towel draped over it) with straight arms as long as possible. Grip endurance for the obstacle course.
Rope climb (wrap method) — trap the rope between the feet (one foot pins it on top of the other), stand up with the legs, slide the hands up, re-pin. Legs climb; arms steer.
Pistol squat progression — a one-leg squat. Start by squatting down to a chair on one leg and standing back up; lower the seat over time. Gym-free leg strength.
Step-up — step onto a knee-high box or bench with one leg, drive up until standing on it, step down under control. Holding dumbbells makes it a full leg exercise.
Towel row — loop a towel around a pole or door handle, lean back holding both ends, pull your chest to your hands. A rowing exercise with zero equipment.
Lift D — the optional 4th lifting day (35 min, upper-body accessories: incline press, rows, laterals, curls, triceps, face pulls). Earned, not default — conditions are in the Adjust tab under "I want to add more training."
Plank — forearms and toes, body a straight line, squeeze everything, breathe. No sagging hips.
Flutter kicks — lying on your back, hands under hips, legs straight and 6 inches up, kick in small alternating pulses.
Side-lying leg lift — lying on your side, lift the top leg to ~45° and lower slowly. Hip stabilizers for running.
Single-leg balance — stand on one foot, eyes closed, 30 seconds. Ankle control that prevents sprains on sand and trails.

Program methods

Morning routine — 3 max-effort push-up sets within ~10 minutes of waking, then the day's pull-up work. Phase A: Mon/Wed/Fri from Week 5. Phase B: every weekday from Week 26.
Small-sets method (grease the groove) — many easy sets of about half your max, spread through the day, never near failure. How pull-ups are built before you can do 8.
Armstrong routine — the 5-day/week pull-up program (Sections tab, 5): Mon max sets, Tue pyramid, Wed three grips, Thu max training sets, Fri repeat the hardest day. The standard military route to 20+.
Pyramid — count 1-2-3-4-5-4-3-2-1; each number = that many pull-ups, double it in push-ups, triple it in sit-ups. Minimal rest.
Grinder — 100 pull-ups, 200 push-ups, 300 sit-ups, each in as few sets as possible, running 400m between every set. Week 37 half size, Week 41 full.
Run-Armor — the twice-weekly 10-minute injury-prevention circuit (Sections tab, 12): slow calves, bent-knee calves, toe raises, single-leg balance, side leg lifts.
Box breathing — in 4 seconds, hold 4, out 4, hold 4. Five minutes nightly and between test events. Trains calm under stress.
Fast reset — two quick sniffs in through the nose, one long slow breath out. Drops heart rate and nerves between hard efforts.
The five knots — bowline, square knot, becket's bend, clove hitch, right angle. Learned dry with a short rope, two 5-minute blocks a week in Phase B. Tested underwater at BUD/S itself — never self-practice the underwater part.
Test protocol — the exact PST sequence with fixed rests (Sections tab, 15): swim, 10 min, push-ups, 2 min, sit-ups, 2 min, pull-ups, 10 min, run.

Military and paperwork terms

PST — Physical Screening Test: 500-yd swim, push-ups, sit-ups, pull-ups, 1.5-mile run. The score sheet that gets you a SEAL contract.
BUD/S — Basic Underwater Demolition/SEAL training in Coronado: the ~6-month selection course (orientation, then three 7-week phases).
Hell Week — the famous ~5.5-day event in First Phase: constant training on ~4 total hours of sleep.
NSW — Naval Special Warfare, the SEAL community's command. Publishes the official guides at sealswcc.com.
SEAL Challenge / SO contract — the enlistment contract that guarantees a shot at BUD/S. SO = Special Warfare Operator, the SEAL job rating.
DEP — Delayed Entry Program: signed but not yet shipped. You take official monthly PSTs during this window; paperwork can start at 17 with a parent's signature.
MEPS — Military Entrance Processing Station: the physical exam, vision test, and ASVAB site.
ASVAB — the military entrance exam. SEAL contract needs GS+MC+EI ≥ 170 or VE+MK+MC+CS ≥ 220 (those letters are its sub-test scores; ask the recruiter for the Coding Speed test).
C-SORT — a one-time computer test of mental resilience taken at recruiting. Cannot be retaken. It measures the exact skills in Sections tab, 14.
NSW coordinator / mentor — the official who administers scored PSTs and guides candidates through DEP. Ask the recruiter to connect you.
NSW Prep — the 8-week course after boot camp with its own exit test: 1,000m fin swim ≤20:00, 4-mile run ≤31:00, 70 push-ups, 60 sit-ups, 10 pull-ups.
